Ethics & Future
Chapter 6 is the conscience of the course. After five chapters showing what AI can do, this chapter asks what AI should do. We'll cover real-world failures (Amazon hiring bias, COMPAS, deepfakes), the EU AI Act's risk framework, governance standards (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001), and practical deployment checklists. End with the Road Ahead — not to predict, but to prepare. The message: responsible AI is not a brake on innovation, it is a steering wheel.
Slide 01 · Known Risks

Bias & Hallucination

Bias

Models learn patterns from human-generated data — including human prejudices.

  • Gender stereotypes in job descriptions
  • Under-representation of minorities
  • Geographic & cultural bias (Western-centric data)
  • Recency bias from training window
Hallucination

Models generate plausible-sounding but false content with full confidence.

  • Invented citations and papers
  • Wrong legal / medical / technical facts
  • Confident errors in code logic
  • Mitigated by: RAG, grounding, verification
Rule: Never use AI output without human review in high-stakes decisions (hiring, medicine, law, finance).
Hallucination rate: GPT-4 hallucinates ~3-5% on factual queries (lower with RAG); older models up to 20%. The dangerous case: confident, fluent, plausible-sounding errors on specialised topics the model rarely saw in training. Solution stack: RAG for facts, chain-of-thought for reasoning, constitutional AI for behaviour constraints, human review for high-stakes decisions. Bias: test your AI outputs with diverse user personas before deployment.

Slide 02 · Governance

EU AI Act &
Governance

EU AI Act — In force Aug 2024

The world's first comprehensive AI law. Risk-based approach with four tiers.

🔴 Unacceptable — Prohibited. Social scoring, subliminal manipulation.
🟠 High Risk — Heavy regulation. Healthcare, employment, education, law enforcement.
🟡 Limited Risk — Transparency required. Chatbots must disclose they are AI.
🟢 Minimal Risk — Free to use. Games, spam filters, AI-generated content.
Global Landscape
  • 🇺🇸 USA — Executive Order on AI Safety (Oct 2023). Voluntary commitments.
  • 🇨🇳 China — Generative AI Regulations since 2023.
  • 🇬🇧 UK — Principles-based approach. Sector-specific regulators.
EU AI Act timeline: General-purpose AI rules apply Feb 2025. High-risk AI requirements by Aug 2026. Companies deploying AI in the EU must comply or face fines up to €35M or 7% of global turnover. Key for architects: any AI touching HR, credit scoring, access to essential services, or law enforcement triggers High Risk classification. Documentation, human oversight, and transparency are not optional — they are legal requirements.

Slide 03 · Case Studies

Real-World
AI Failures

Amazon Hiring Tool (2018)

CV screening model penalised female candidates. Trained on 10 years of male-dominated hiring data — learned gender was a negative signal.

COMPAS Recidivism (2016)

Criminal risk scoring system was twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as high-risk compared to white defendants.

Lawyer Cites Fake Cases (2023)

A New York attorney used ChatGPT for legal research. The AI invented six fake case citations — complete with made-up judges and rulings. Sanctions followed.

Deepfake Fraud ($25M, 2024)

Hong Kong firm employee tricked by AI-generated video call impersonating the CFO. Result: $25 million wire transfer to scammers.

Lesson: Every failure above was preventable with proper governance: bias audits, human oversight, output verification, and authentication protocols.
Amazon scrapped the tool after discovering gender bias. COMPAS study by ProPublica sparked national debate on algorithmic fairness. The lawyer case (Mata v. Avianca) became a landmark warning — always verify AI-generated citations. The Hong Kong deepfake: attackers cloned voices and faces from public videos. Takeaway: AI failures are rarely technical surprises — they're governance failures. The technology worked exactly as trained; the problem was what it was trained on and how it was deployed without safeguards.

Slide 04 · Frameworks

AI Governance
Frameworks

NIST AI RMF 1.0 (2023)
  • Govern — Policies, roles, culture of risk awareness
  • Map — Identify context, stakeholders, potential harms
  • Measure — Quantify risks with metrics and benchmarks
  • Manage — Prioritise, respond, monitor, improve
ISO/IEC 42001 (2023)
  • First international standard for AI Management Systems
  • Certifiable — aligns with ISO 27001 (InfoSec)
  • Covers full AI lifecycle: design → deploy → retire
🛡️ Pre-Deployment Checklist
☑ Data audit — Training data reviewed for bias, IP, privacy
☑ Eval suite — Accuracy, fairness metrics, adversarial tests
☑ Human oversight — Escalation paths, override controls
☑ Incident plan — Response playbook for AI failures
☑ Documentation — Model card, data sheet, risk assessment
☑ Monitoring — Drift detection, feedback loops, re-training triggers
NIST AI RMF: voluntary but often used as the baseline for US federal agencies and becoming the de facto standard for enterprise AI governance. The four functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) mirror the NIST Cybersecurity Framework structure — familiar territory for InfoSec teams. ISO 42001: published Dec 2023, first certifiable AI standard. Companies pursuing this demonstrate to regulators and customers that they have systematic AI governance. The pre-deployment checklist is a simplified version of what large tech companies already do internally — the key is making it standard practice for every organization deploying AI, not just Big Tech.

Slide 05 · Responsible AI

Responsible AI
in Practice

Transparency
  • Disclose when AI is in use
  • Explain model decisions (XAI)
  • Document training data sources
Fairness
  • Audit for demographic bias
  • Diverse training data and teams
  • Measure disparate impact
Safety & Control
  • Human-in-the-loop for high stakes
  • Kill switch & rollback plan
  • Red-team before deployment
Principle: Responsible AI is not a compliance checkbox — it is a product quality standard.
Responsible AI frameworks: Google's PAIR, Microsoft's Responsible AI Standard, IBM's AI Fairness 360. Red-teaming: deliberately trying to break your AI system before attackers do. Prompt injection, jailbreaks, data poisoning. For product managers: every AI feature needs a 'what if this goes wrong' decision tree. For architects: design for explainability from day one — retrofitting XAI is expensive.

Slide 06 · Future

The Road Ahead

Near-term (2025–2027)
  • 🤖 Autonomous AI agents become mainstream in enterprise
  • 🔊 Multi-modal AI: text, voice, image, video in one model
  • 📉 Smaller, faster, on-device models (edge AI)
  • ⚖️ AI regulation expands globally (post-EU AI Act)
Medium-term (2027–2030)
  • 🧠 Reasoning AI: models that genuinely plan and verify (o3-class)
  • 💊 AI in drug discovery, materials, climate simulation
  • 🎓 Personalised education at scale
  • 🤝 Human-AI collaboration as the default work mode
The constant: Technology will keep accelerating. The skill that endures is knowing how to work with AI — critically, creatively, and responsibly.
Key message to close on: don't be afraid of AI, but don't be naive. The jobs that will be displaced are the ones that can be fully described as a repeatable pattern. The jobs that grow are those requiring judgment, empathy, creativity, and ethical reasoning — things that are hard to automate. The best preparation: stay curious, experiment, build things, and develop a critical relationship with AI tools.
